Core Value Propositions

Price Efficiency

The CEA/EUA price gap (~7:1) means NIHA can source CEA cheaply from Chinese entities and swap it into EUA for EU clients at 3–8% below direct EUA exchange purchase price. EU entities fund the acquisition; NIHA handles sourcing and custody.

Access Bridge

EU participants have zero access to Chinese carbon markets. Chinese entities face barriers to EU ETS. NIHA bridges both markets through direct bilateral relationships with Chinese CEA holders, completely bypassing exchange restrictions on both sides.

Full-Service Intermediation

NIHA purchases CEA directly from Chinese entities (not from exchanges), executes the CEA→EUA swap on its own account, takes possession of the EUA, and only then transfers the EUA to the EU client. The EU entity never touches CEA — it simply funds the operation and receives EUA.

Execution Speed & Confidentiality

All transactions are bilateral and off-exchange — invisible to public orderbooks. A 100K-tonne trade executes at a known price with no market impact, no slippage, and no signal to competitors. The platform operates 24/7 with T+0 settlement.

Swap Mechanics

Swap ratio is ~8.5 CEA per 1 EUA (derived from spot prices minus NIHA spread). The swap ratio floats with market prices and is updated continuously from scraped reference data. NIHA's spread on the swap (∼0.5–2%) is the primary revenue source. Critically: NIHA executes the swap on its own account, settles the EUA delivery, takes possession, and only after that transfers the EUA to the EU client. The EU entity is never exposed to CEA or Chinese market mechanics.

Why This Flywheel Doesn't Exist on China ETS

The Shanghai exchange is structurally capped: only ~2,200 compliance entities can trade, foreign access is blocked, there are no market makers, no derivatives, and only 4 hours of trading per day. No amount of demand can widen that funnel — the rules prevent it. NIHA faces none of these constraints. Every new bilateral relationship the platform establishes adds permanent, repeatable liquidity that the exchange simply cannot match.

The Core Thesis

China's carbon exchange is constrained by design: closed access, limited hours, no market makers, no derivatives. These aren't problems that will be fixed quickly — they are structural features of a nascent, heavily regulated market. NIHA doesn't compete with the exchange; it routes around its limitations. Every participant who discovers that NIHA offers better pricing, faster execution, or access they couldn't get otherwise becomes a permanent source of liquidity. The result is a platform that — despite being younger and smaller in absolute terms — is already more dynamic, more accessible, and functionally more liquid for the participants it serves than the regulated market it draws from.

What "More Liquid" Means in Practice

A CEA seller on NIHA can execute a 500,000-tonne block trade instantly at a known price, at any hour, with no market impact. On the Shanghai exchange, the same trade would take 2–3 days, move the price 2–3%, and be visible to every other participant. A EU buyer on NIHA can acquire EUA-equivalent exposure through CEA→swap at a 3–8% discount with no exchange membership. On ICE, the same trade requires clearing infrastructure and moves the tape. Liquidity isn't just volume — it's the ability to transact without friction. By that measure, NIHA is already the more liquid venue for cross-border carbon credit flow.

Key Questions

Common questions about the NIHA model, market mechanics, and why certain alternatives don't work the way you'd expect.

Why wouldn't an EU entity just buy EUA directly from a non-EU holder? Q1

They can, and sometimes they do. If both parties have EU registry accounts, a direct bilateral OTC trade is perfectly legal. The problem is price:

A direct EUA purchase — whether on exchange or bilateral — costs market price (~€81/t). Going through NIHA costs ~€78–79/t effective. The 3–8% saving comes from the CEA→EUA swap arbitrage path that only NIHA can offer.

No non-EU holder will sell EUA at a discount. Why would they? They can sell at €81 on ICE. NIHA's advantage isn't finding a cheaper EUA seller — it's bypassing the EUA market entirely by sourcing cheap CEA (€11.40/t) from Chinese entities and swapping it into EUA.

Beyond price, direct bilateral trades have practical friction:

Discovery — How does an EU steel plant find a HK trading house with surplus EUA? There's no public directory of willing sellers.
Trust — Who sends first? Neither party knows the other. Settlement requires legal agreements (ISDA/EFET), KYC/AML due diligence.
Infrastructure — Both parties need EU registry accounts and settlement coordination. The non-EU entity may not have one.
Price negotiation — Without a market maker, agreeing on price is slow and adversarial.

NIHA solves all of these — but the primary reason is economics: the CEA arbitrage path delivers EUA cheaper than any direct purchase can.

Can Hong Kong entities access the China ETS to trade CEA? Q2

No. Despite Hong Kong's status as a Chinese SAR, HK-based entities are not compliance entities under the national ETS and therefore cannot trade CEA on the Shanghai Environment & Energy Exchange.

The China ETS restricts trading exclusively to the ~2,200 domestic companies directly regulated under the system (currently power sector, expanding to cement/steel/aluminum). Hong Kong companies, foreign companies, financial institutions, and individual investors are all excluded.

There is ongoing work on a "Greater Bay Area Carbon Connect" scheme (similar to Stock Connect for equities), but it remains at the feasibility assessment stage with no specific timeline. HKEx launched Core Climate for voluntary credits, but that does not include CEA.

This means HK entities are in the same position as EU entities — they need NIHA to access CEA. This further expands NIHA's addressable market beyond Europe to include the entire Hong Kong financial and corporate ecosystem.

Why don't Chinese entities just sell CEA on their own exchange? Q3

They can, but the experience is often poor:

Thin liquidity — average daily volume is ~830K tonnes. A 500K-tonne sell order is 60% of daily volume and would depress the price 2–3%.
Wide spreads — 1–2% typical, widening to 3–5% on thin days. That's direct money lost vs. a bilateral deal.
Limited hours — only 4 hours/day of trading (09:30–11:30, 13:00–15:00).
Seasonal demand — volume spikes near compliance deadlines and collapses otherwise. Selling outside the deadline window is painful.
RMB only — proceeds stay in local currency. Via NIHA, sellers receive EUR (hard currency diversification).

NIHA, backed by EU demand, can pay a premium to the domestic exchange price. For a Chinese seller, getting ¥101–102 equivalent via NIHA vs ¥97–99 on exchange (after slippage) is a 3–5% improvement — plus EUR diversification and instant execution.
What prevents someone from replicating NIHA's model? Q4

The model is conceptually simple but operationally difficult to replicate:

Bilateral relationships with Chinese CEA holders — These are built over years through trust, local presence, and industry networks. You cannot source CEA from a directory or an API. Each relationship is a handshake deal with a compliance entity.
Cross-border regulatory navigation — Operating across EU, Chinese, and HK regulatory frameworks simultaneously requires specialized legal and compliance infrastructure.
Swap execution capability — Actually converting CEA to EUA requires inventory management, registry access on both sides, and settlement infrastructure that takes significant capital and time to build.
Network effects — Once NIHA has liquidity, each new participant makes it more attractive. A new entrant starts with zero liquidity and zero counterparties — the cold-start problem is severe in bilateral OTC markets.

The key insight is that NIHA simulates an OTC dark pool, not a public exchange.
